# Group Drive / Unit Drive Fact Check

Purpose: keep a reusable reference for the Slide 4 explanation in `speaker-script-spoken.zh.md`.

## Short Answer

For a 10-minute casual sharing, it is reasonable to explain the difference as:

> group drive = many machines tied to one central power-transmission system; unit drive = each machine has its own motor and can be arranged/controlled more independently.

This is faithful enough for the talk. The shaft and belt are still historically important, but they do not need to be the main spoken point. They are the physical reason the old machines were tied together.

## Safer Wording

Avoid saying:

> The whole factory had only one steam engine because steam engines were too big to have more than one.

That is too absolute. A safer version:

> 舊工廠通常是一台或少數幾台原動機,透過傳動軸和皮帶,把動力分給很多機器。重點不是只有蒸汽機本身,而是整個廠房被同一套中央動力系統綁住。

## What The Sources Say

- Devine describes the shift from water/steam prime movers with shaft-and-belt systems to electric motors driving individual machines. His key claim is that electricity reduced energy use, but more importantly enabled more output per unit of capital and labor.
- David, quoting DuBoff, says early electrification often kept belts and shafting: plants installed electric motors to drive separate shafting sections, and this group drive was initially cheaper than a full unit-drive system because the existing steam-engine/shafting apparatus could be retained.
- David's list of unit-drive advantages includes better machine control, easier speed changes, freer machine placement and materials handling, modular maintenance, cleaner/lighter factories, and lower risks from belts and overhead transmission equipment.
- A 1910 engineering article on motor drive lists similar practical advantages: easier machine/material arrangement, the ability to shut down departments without disturbing others, lower repair/maintenance from removing shafts/pulleys/belts/gears, and broader possibilities for work design.

## Talk-Safe Interpretation

The best 10-minute version is:

> The contrast is not "shaft bad, motor good." The contrast is "central coupled system" versus "distributed, independently controllable machines." Shafts and belts matter because they created the coupling, but the audience only needs to remember the coupling.

So the revised Slide 4 line can be:

> 生產力不是因為「有電」就自動冒出來,而是電讓工廠有機會把原本綁在一起的生產方式拆開,重新設計。

## Terminology Note

Use:

- `group drive` / `line-shaft drive`
- `unit drive` / `individual drive`

Do not use `web drive` for this point. In search results, `web drive` mostly refers to machinery that drives a web of material, such as paper, film, or printing/copier mechanisms. It is not the David/Devine factory-electrification contrast.
